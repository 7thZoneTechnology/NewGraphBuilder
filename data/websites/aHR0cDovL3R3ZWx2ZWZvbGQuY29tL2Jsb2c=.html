Redefining Brand Safety

by Brian McAllister, VP Revenue Optimization

Oct 20, 2014  |   Posted in The Fold: 12F Blog

Buying digital media is too often like Forrest Gump’s box of chocolates: “You never know what you’re gonna get.” We work in an ocean of black boxes. Whether it’s ad networks or run-of-section buys from publishers, buyers rarely know where their ads will run. Even Google’s Preferred Video package -- advertised as YouTube’s best of the best -- offers only a sample of representative channels, not the actual clips your pre-roll will precede. We define “transparency” differently at Twelvefold. We show agencies and brands where their ads will appear -- down to the page level -- before, during and after a campaign. We’re wired to be that accountable, since our goal is getting the right ad to the right user at the right time, in a way other targeting technologies cannot. Given how hard we work to find the right pages for each campaign, we don’t want to see the resulting impressions wasted on anyone other than an engaged consumer. So we take a particularly hard line on inventory quality. We believe the exchanges must ultimately take and own responsibility for rooting out fraud and ensuring a consistent, high-quality supply. Until that happens, we employ a series of tactics to ensure safety for our customers. First, we limit all buys to our whitelist of 40,000 brand-safe websites. This eliminates an enormous amount of noise, right off the bat. Fly-by-night domains, created for the sole purpose of clicking on advertisements, have no way of breaking into the set of pages we’re matching and buying. Second, we routinely go back and make sure the sites on our whitelist don’t contain obscene or pornographic material, spyware or malware, excessive profanity, or promote illegal activities. We also measure viewability for all impressions, to assess the impact on performance and to make optimization decisions accordingly. Third, we work with a series of independent parties, who watch for suspicious signals that suggest fraud. For example: unusually-high click-through rates, abnormal traffic from a single web browser, or concentrated visits from certain IP addresses. This tactic has proven particularly helpful to combat “spoofing,” when illegitimate websites hide under reputable publishers’ domain names. Too often, these forensic solutions are used to report on fraudulent impressions, versus preventing them in the first place. But what use is knowing after the fact, other than to get a credit or make-good? We challenged our partners to rig their technology for real-time screening of individual impressions, so we’d know whether opportunities are legitimate before we bid on them. The exchanges tell us very few buyers are as proactive and aggressive as Twelvefold in this regard. Thinking back to the proverbial box of chocolates: we open it up, show you what’s inside, and give you a legend, so you don’t taste mint when you were expecting raspberry. Then we cut open each piece to be sure it’s not hollow. This is what we think it ought to look like, when someone tells you they take brand safety seriously. Too many ad tech companies bolt third-party fraud reporting onto their black boxes and call themselves “transparent.” But at the end of the day, they still can’t and won’t tell you where your campaign is running. That’s like handing you the closed box of chocolates, saying “Trust us, we’ve taken out the duds.” You deserve better. We designed our platform and business model so we wouldn’t be threatened or need to react as waste, fraud and inefficiency get squeezed out of this business. That’s given us a lot of free time to focus on things that really matter, like helping brands hit their marketing objectives out of the park.

Transparency is cheap compared to the alternative

by Dave Hills

May 08, 2014  |   Posted in The Fold: 12F Blog

How many times are we going to set ourselves back with huge and important brands by avoiding total industry transparency? We should all hope this is the last time since it's costing all of us money, time and wasted resources having to remedially fix a problem that with the flip of a switch--and business model--could be stomped out today. And for good.

We've got the resources as an industry to do it, and it’s time to do it now. Not next year, not over a period of time, and please let's not kick the can down the road so we can all get out with our dough and leave it for the next guy. All that does is invoke the one step forward and two steps back rule.

Transparency isn't tricky or difficult to do. In fact, avoiding transparency seems to be a very expensive endeavor. You have to build a black box, conjure up some fancy language around the benefits of the box, and then deploy it, asking customers just to trust we know what we're doing. Then you have to deal with the fallout of the black box, like when paid ads run on pirating sites. Imagine all the time resources wasted over the next few days when good brands and good companies have to figure out how it happened and how to prevent it going forward. And as we speak there are new companies being formed to "protect" us from ourselves. Verification and exposing issues has its place but we really shouldn’t need those things to begin with.

Prior to having automated, impression level, real time evaluation capabilities like we do today, we could likely rationalize that the dark corners of the industry existed and there wasn't much you could do about it. But that is no longer the case. We all have access to a very brightly-lit view of impressions in real time through exchanges. That huge stream of very visible impressions get evaluated, valued and actioned in real time. And if you can't see where the impression comes from, simply don’t make the placement. It’s that easy to do.

It's time, folks, and it has to be us that do it. Who's "us"? Brands that need to push partners to move toward total transparency now. Those partners then have to adopt transparency in their own shop and refuse to deal with a lack of it. The exchanges have to reward publishers who show exactly what's being put in auction and not reward those who mask impressions. The buyers then have to reject all of the masked impressions. If the sites don't get revenue from masking then it will stop.

And please--please--let's stop blaming the other guy. Finger pointing has no place in a medium that aspires to unseat all other media with ad spend growth.

We did it, and we're proud to be 100% transparent. As a result, our customer conversations are about what's next--not what just happened.

Real Time Marketing Will Result in Real Time Budgets

by Dave Hills

Apr 02, 2014  |   Posted in The Fold: 12F Blog

Each day now, brands can look at real time trends available through an ever increasing supply of tools, perspective and data. It's incredible to watch agencies and clients light up when they find a "eureka" moment. That flow of data that shows them something about their customers, weakness in a competitor, a trend of consumer usage that can be capitalized on, or any other signal that shows where best to set the hook for the best catch.

What comes next are real time budgets for those tools, as well as companies that can both identify an important stream of data and then help the brand take immediate advantage of it. Not later. Not tomorrow. Now. The ability to push a "buy" button and then tame the stream of real time impressions to accomplish a campaign or broader brand goal is the next logical step in the process that leads us to a real time world of data streams that are conquered and not just analyzed. It's the combination of intelligence and ability to activate in the moment that takes real time that's interesting to real time that's powerful. Mindshare's "LOOP" is one take on this. An agency-wide ability to measure real time trends and then push them along to brands will become a wider trend.

Right now, the beginnings of infrastructure on top of the real time exchanges are giving us glimpses of what's possible and it's very exciting. The openness of the real time infrastructure is allowing companies, agencies and brands to build real time analysis layers all with a ubiquitous "buy" button to capture that moment in time where heat develops energy. The next piece will be brands and agencies setting budgets behind the "buy" button. Already there are several multi-brand companies that have developed a portfolio budget. Here, there's allocation to support individual brands and with a pool of spend left that can be applied to real time opportunities for a brand or a series of brands. This will allow for immediate action--within overall goals--so brands will not lose the opportunity by having weeks of approvals needed. With the old model, by the time things get approved the opportunity has passed.

Now we're cooking with gas, as it were, and not firewood. Real time data, packaged in a digestible and appealing fashion, shows a trend that is happening now with an easy way to push budget against it. If we do that then we'll finally be on the same page with the consumer, who is saying: I'm ready now, please don't make me wait.

Big Data Demands, Big Data Management Concepts

by Dave Hills

Jan 13, 2014  |   Posted in The Fold: 12F Blog

It's a blizzard out there in a very cold world and that's not just about the weather covering most of the country during the beginning of the year. It's an apt metaphor for the Big Data management challenge facing the industry. Snow blowing all over, making it hard to see the lines in the road, winds buffeting everyone trying to get somewhere and people staying inside rather than going out to face the elements.

The problem is you just can't ignore the need to harness and manage big data. The Polar Vortex will retreat back to the Arctic. The need to effectively manage Big Data won't go away until the industry takes stands to provide both brands and consumers with value through better ad targeting using Big Data.

Acxiom and other companies are doing incredible work around how to not only manage, but also how to model for forecasting, and how to predict a consumers next move based on what they've been up to. It might appear on the surface as creepy but as long as opt in and opt out is a real possibility for consumers, then they can effectively give consent. That's the first order of business. If you want to track me I'm ok with that but you better give me a chance to say yes or no. Over time, privacy concerns that are properly addressed become privacy choices, and that's the first point on the horizon to shoot for.

Then there's the ability to actually leverage multiple data points--such as online behavior, purchase history and third party data--into one smooth stream of data that actually realizes I don't want to buy the handbag referenced in this article. I'm simply not the customer and chasing me each day for months isn't going to change the fact that I don't want a handbag. Behavioral targeting has a place in the industry but it can't be the only place.

The other place that Big Data has a big upside is in understanding the moment of consumption I'm in right now. Understanding where I am and what I'm consuming from content--and why I'm consuming it--is a tremendous indicator of my intent. And it doesn't require any personal information, any tracking of my history or any information on my purchases. It's simply a very deep and very fast analysis of what I'm looking at, and then understanding why I'm looking at it.

This is the development and deployment of Mindset Marketing based simply on understanding why I'm looking at the content I'm consuming. Understanding why I'm consuming it along with what the content is about, and doing that at the instant of consumption, can be as good as or superior to tracking all of my behaviors and purchases.

It's Big Data deployed at the instant of consumption and it complements any method of behavioral targeting that uses history. The faster we embrace multiple ways of managing Big Data, the better off we and consumers will be.

Is retargeting fighting this war with the last war’s tactics?

by Dave Hills

Nov 22, 2013  |   Posted in The Fold: 12F Blog

In theory, retargeting can and should be very, very effective--if done properly. Retargeting, search or otherwise, has become a very blunt instrument in developing KPI's for any campaign. In a last click attribution world, it created some very bad, brand unsafe habits in terms of where ads run in order to grab the attribution. With that world ending soon, the questions are: what place does retargeting have going forward and how should it be deployed?

One very simple thing to do is to follow a brands attribution window. If a site uses a 14 day attribution window, for instance, then stop retargeting the person when that window closes. The window must be there for some reason, and if that reason is good enough for attribution then it should be good enough for retargeting.

Then there's the general topic of cleaning segments as providers go along and transparency. Transparency hawks provide tools that are 100% transparent to the impression level. Whichever companies are providing retargeting services should be transparent and make sure the segments are cleaned regularly. Scale becomes more difficult when people are removed at regular intervals, but keeping things stale to preserve scale isn't the way to go. It's simply not effective.

At Twelvefold, we focus all of our time and effort on the moment of consumption and understanding the mindset the content creates with its audience while it's being consumed. Spectrum was built under the assumption that if we understand what you're thinking, by simply understanding the content, at the moment you're consuming it, our chances of serving a relevant ad goes up substantially. Our customers agree.

We're in a real time, impression based world that demands transparency. It's a new day and a new war. We shouldn't fight it with the last war's weapons, strategies or tactics.

Brand safety needs to be built into a company’s DNA

by Dave Hills

Oct 03, 2013  |   Posted in The Fold: 12F Blog

Twelvefold takes transparency seriously, which is why we've been following the Adweek series on fraud. We highly recommend reading the pieces they've written so far this year. It gives a very good sense of how prevalent fraud is in today’s ad stack as well as the level of sophistication involved.

This article made its way around the sales floor here this morning and our Director of East Coast Sales Kaitlin Keaveney reminded us that fraud is one thing we need to guard against. She also detailed a series of elements that create an environment of brand safety, which is critical to keeping great brands coming back to 12Fold. In fact, they already do and that is in large measure due to the environment of trust we've built through both platform and business practices.

Brand safety is only accomplished if it is a stated goal of the company. That goal has to be executed through a combination of providing a proprietary platform, using other technology tools available and--most importantly--adhering to business practices that makes brands and their agencies want to be associated with your company. Here's our view of the formula for success.

The company platform has to orient itself toward brand safety. We designed two things into Spectrum that create a foundation of brand safety. First, we don't use cookies or any form of user targeting, so we’re able to avoid cookie or pixel fraud. Second, we designed Spectrum to be totally transparent. That means our customers know where they are going to run before we launch a campaign and we show them where they ran at the end. We built these two features into the platform because we saw a world where audiences can be targeted without cookies and one where transparency would be demanded by agencies and brands.

We also use syndicated tools and software to augment what we do for things like viewability and other critical hygiene measures. We like tools that offer third party validation of our approach or add to our optimization and analytic capabilities. For one, they give our customers an outsider’s view of our effectiveness. Plus, these tools augment what Spectrum already does, allowing us to focus on developing new and exciting features for the platform. We're perfectly happy when a really innovative company comes up with software we don't have to develop and maintain. It just means more time for us to spend on understanding the mindset content creates with its audience!

And of course there are business practices. That foundation comes from people, and we hire and retain talent that is both high-performing and highly-ethical. Our main business focus is our "buyable index". That's the list of sites we've vetted to have real content and a very good brand advertising experience. It's not simple or cheap to vet tens of thousands of sites but it’s worth it in the name of brand safety.

The Ad Week fraud series reminds us that it’s important to be ever-vigilant, because gaming this industry to make money through fraudulent practices is essentially a nuclear arms race. We as an industry beat it back and then the unethical players make improvements. It has happened since I entered the industry in 1994.

The good news is that the entire ad stack moving toward our view of the world and our way of doing business. We see the new entrants into the ecosystem fostering greater transparency and controls around brand safety, and we welcome them to the neighborhood.

Creative Content + Real-Time Technology = Engaged Audience

by Dave Hills

Jan 20, 2012  |   Posted in The Fold: 12F Blog

Advertisers are interested in finding the key to connecting with what consumers are thinking and feeling. Why? Consumers are increasingly expressive online but also quicker to dismiss brand messages that lack relevance to their interests. Emotion drives consumers to act, and advertisers know they must understand this sentiment to drive engagement.

Today’s online advertising real-time capabilities enable brands to deliver messages valuable to the audience at the very moment they’re consuming content, such reading a blog post or online news article. Content creates an emotional response with its reader, and brands can create a better ad experience and connection with an audience by matching creative ad messages to content that has meaning to the reader, in real-time.

But content is fluid and constantly changing, as is the location and intent of target audiences, which is where technology such as Spectrum enters the picture. For example, we can see the actual URL as content is curated, score it, know if that particular page is relevant to the brand’s target audience, and even the reader’s potential intent. With an understanding of content, the environment, and emotive state of the reader, marketers can develop creative campaigns with conditional triggers to drive audience engagement and action.
